The Origins of Checkers: A Historical Journey

By Alexis Briggs on August 3, 2025

The origins of checkers, a game enjoyed across cultures and generations, are surprisingly deep and complex. It’s a game that seems almost inherently simple, yet its evolution stretches back thousands of years, intertwined with the histories of ancient civilizations. This article will trace this fascinating journey, from its ancient roots to the standardized form we know and love today.

Ancient Ancestors: Senet and Mehen

The direct lineage of checkers begins with ancient board games that emerged in the Near East and Egypt. While not strictly checkers, these games laid the groundwork for the movement and strategic thinking that would eventually define it. The most prominent predecessor is undoubtedly Senet, a game played in Egypt as early as 3100 BC. Senet was far more than just a game; it held profound religious and symbolic significance. The board represented the journey of the soul through the afterlife, and the pieces’ movements mirrored the challenges and trials faced during this perilous voyage. Though the rules are debated, Senet involved moving pieces along a board, aiming to reach the opposite end, and incorporating elements of dice rolls that introduced an element of chance.

Around the same time, Mehen was prevalent in ancient Egypt. Mehen, meaning "belly," features a board with a serpent-like design, reflecting the Egyptian belief in the primordial serpent god Apep. While the rules of Mehen are less clearly documented than Senet, scholars believe it involved moving pieces in a similar fashion, often with a focus on capturing opponent’s pieces. The connection between Senet and Mehen is significant: both demonstrate a preoccupation with structured movement, strategic positioning, and the aspiration to “reach the end” – themes that would become central to the development of checkers.

Diffusion Through the Ancient World

From Egypt, the game’s influence spread throughout the ancient world. The Greeks adopted a game called Episkyros around the 6th century BC. Episkyros shared many similarities with Senet and Episkyros incorporated elements of dice, adding to its complexity and strategic depth. Greek historians, such as Plato and Aristotle, discussed Episkyros, cementing its status as a popular pastime. Archaeological evidence, including game boards discovered in Greek settlements, provides tangible proof of its widespread play.

The Romans, heavily influenced by the Greeks, also embraced Episkyros, adapting it into a game known as Ludus Latrunculorum. The Romans added new elements, such as the concept of “guarding” pieces, which significantly influenced the development of checkers’ defensive strategies. Roman military training even incorporated variations of this game, suggesting its perceived value in developing tactical thinking and strategic foresight.

Modern Checkers Emerges: Alquerque and Draughts

As the Roman Empire declined, the game continued to evolve, taking on distinct regional forms. In Spain and Portugal, the game became known as Alquerque. Alquerque is widely considered the direct ancestor of modern checkers. The board was smaller, and the rules were simpler than those of Episkyros, but the core mechanics of jumping and capturing remained intact. The name "Alquerque" derives from the city of Alquerque in Portugal, where the game was particularly popular.

Across Europe, the game was often referred to as “Draughts” (or “Tafl” in Wales). "Draughts" is a term that emerged in England during the 12th century and remained dominant for centuries. The rules of Draughts evolved over time, with variations existing across different regions. Notably, the rules governing the initial placement of pieces and the rules regarding "crowning" (promoting a piece to a more powerful form) differed considerably from those practiced elsewhere.

The standardization of checkers, as we know it today, is largely attributed to the United States in the 19th century. The American checkers rules, established in 1891, introduced the modern 12-point board, simplified the crowning rules, and standardized the piece movements. This version became the basis for the internationally recognized rules of checkers.

Checkers Today

Today, checkers is played worldwide, enjoyed by people of all ages. Despite its ancient origins, the game retains its core appeal – a blend of strategy, tactics, and a touch of chance. Organizations like the World Draughts Federation oversee international competitions, ensuring the game’s continued popularity and preservation. From the ancient rituals surrounding Senet to the competitive games played today, the story of checkers is a remarkable testament to the enduring human fascination with strategic board games. It truly is a game that has spanned millennia, connecting cultures and offering endless opportunities for intellectual challenge and enjoyment.
